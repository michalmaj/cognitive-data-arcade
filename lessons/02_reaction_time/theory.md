# Theory: Reaction Time

## 1. What Reaction Time Measures

Reaction time (RT) is the interval between the presentation of a stimulus and the initiation of a response. It is measured in milliseconds (ms). Despite its apparent simplicity, RT reflects at least three distinct cognitive processes:

- **Signal detection** — the sensory system registers the stimulus (a light, a sound, a touch).
- **Decision** — the brain identifies the stimulus and selects the appropriate response.
- **Motor execution** — the motor system translates the decision into a physical movement.

The total RT is the sum of these components. By varying task conditions, researchers can add or remove one component and measure the time cost of that operation alone.

## 2. Donders' Subtraction Method

In 1868, Franciscus Donders developed the first systematic method for measuring mental processes. His insight: measure RT in two conditions that differ by exactly one mental operation, then subtract the simpler RT from the more complex one. The difference is the time cost of the added operation.

For example:
- **Simple RT**: a light turns on → press a button as fast as possible. This measures detection + motor execution.
- **Choice RT**: a red or green light turns on → press the left button for red, the right button for green. This measures detection + decision + motor execution.

If simple RT averages 200 ms and choice RT averages 400 ms, the decision step costs approximately 200 ms. This was the first experimental evidence that mental processes take measurable time — a foundational result for cognitive psychology.

## 3. RT Distributions

A single RT measurement is noisy. The mean of many trials is more informative, but even the mean can be misleading. RT distributions have characteristic shapes:

- **Right skew** — most responses cluster near the lower bound (fast), but occasional slow responses extend the distribution rightward. The median is often a better summary than the mean.
- **Practice effects** — RT decreases across a session as participants warm up. The first few trials are typically slower.
- **Outliers** — responses faster than 100 ms are anticipations, not true reactions. Responses slower than 1000 ms often reflect distraction. Both distort the mean.

A typical simple-RT histogram shows a peak around 200–300 ms with a long rightward tail.

## 4. Typical Human RT Values

| Condition | Typical RT |
|---|---|
| Simple visual RT | 180–250 ms |
| Simple auditory RT | 140–200 ms |
| Choice RT (2 options) | 350–500 ms |
| Stroop (congruent) | 500–700 ms |
| Stroop (incongruent) | 700–1000 ms |

These values apply to young adults in alert, rested conditions. RT increases with age, fatigue, alcohol, and cognitive load.

## 5. What Affects Reaction Time

Several factors reliably modulate RT:

- **Fatigue** — sleep deprivation increases RT by 10–30% and also increases variability.
- **Caffeine** — moderate doses (100–200 mg) reduce RT by approximately 10 ms on average.
- **Age** — simple RT peaks in early adulthood (~25 years) and increases by ~1 ms per year thereafter.
- **Practice** — RT decreases systematically across dozens of trials, then stabilises.
- **Cognitive load** — performing a secondary task simultaneously increases RT on both tasks.

Understanding these factors explains why RT measurement requires controlled conditions: the same participant may produce very different RT distributions on different days.

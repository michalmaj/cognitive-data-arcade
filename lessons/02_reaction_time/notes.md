## Expected RT Ranges

Students typically produce simple RTs in the range of 200–350 ms. Values outside this range are not errors:
- Values below 200 ms are common for practiced participants or those who anticipate the stimulus.
- Values above 400 ms often reflect unfamiliarity with the response key or brief distraction during the session.

If a student's mean exceeds 500 ms, suggest repeating the session after a brief rest.

## Handling Outliers

Students sometimes worry that slow trials indicate "something is wrong." It is useful to clarify that 2–5 outlier trials per 60-trial session are entirely normal. The histogram's right tail is not a measurement error — it is a genuine feature of RT distributions that researchers account for routinely.

Common causes of outlier trials:
- Brief inattention (attention lapsed for a fraction of a second)
- Uncertainty about which key to press
- Deliberate verification of the stimulus before responding

## Suggested Answers

**Q1 — Right-skew**
RT distributions are right-skewed because fast responses are bounded from below (the nervous system cannot respond faster than ~100 ms due to signal transmission time), while slow responses are unbounded — any distraction adds time. This asymmetry is a property of the process, not measurement noise.

**Q2 — Sleepless night**
After sleep deprivation: mean RT increases, variance increases more, and the slow tail extends further. Fast trials remain approximately fast (the lower bound is unchanged by fatigue), but the proportion of slow trials increases substantially.

**Q3 — Donders and the window into the mind**
Donders assumed that mental stages are discrete, sequential, and additively independent (the "pure insertion" assumption): adding a decision stage inserts time without affecting the other stages. Later research showed this assumption is imperfect — stages can overlap and interact. However, the subtraction method remains a useful approximation and historically established cognitive psychology as a quantitative discipline.

# Theory: Big Data in Cognitive Science

## 1. What Is Big Data?

Big Data refers to datasets so large or complex that traditional data-processing tools cannot handle them efficiently. Three properties define Big Data, known as the three Vs:

- **Volume** — the sheer amount of data. In cognitive science, the Human Connectome Project collected brain-imaging data from 1,200 participants at resolutions that produce terabytes per scan session.
- **Velocity** — how fast data arrives. EEG sensors record electrical brain activity at 256–2048 samples per second; a one-hour session generates millions of data points.
- **Variety** — the diversity of formats. Cognitive researchers simultaneously handle fMRI images, reaction-time spreadsheets, speech recordings, GPS coordinates from smartphones, and free-text clinical notes.

Understanding these three properties explains why cognitive science transformed from small laboratory studies into a data-driven discipline over the last two decades.

## 2. A Historical Perspective

The story begins in 1868, when the Dutch physician Franciscus Donders measured how long it takes the human mind to make a decision. He subtracted one reaction-time measurement from another — a technique requiring as few as 20 participants. His dataset fit on a single sheet of paper.

Fast-forward to 2010: the Human Connectome Project begins mapping every connection in the human brain for 1,200 participants. A single participant's diffusion MRI scan occupies several gigabytes. Donders' subtraction method — now computerised — is still used, but embedded inside datasets measured in terabytes.

Today, digital phenotyping pushes further: smartphones silently record hundreds of variables (GPS location, keystroke speed, sleep duration, social-media activity) around the clock for thousands of participants. Data volumes have grown by a factor of roughly one billion since Donders' original experiments.

## 3. Open Datasets in Cognitive Science

Several landmark open datasets have reshaped what questions researchers can ask:

**Human Connectome Project (HCP)** — structural and functional MRI data from 1,200 healthy adults. Mapping white-matter tracts between brain regions required participants, hardware, and computational resources unimaginable in the twentieth century. The dataset is openly available to registered researchers worldwide.

**OpenNeuro.org** — a public repository hosting thousands of MRI and EEG datasets. Researchers deposit data after publication, creating a growing archive that others can re-analyse without running new experiments.

**CHILDES** (Child Language Data Exchange System) — audio and video recordings of children's speech across dozens of languages and cultures. Used to study how humans acquire language, a process too slow and subtle to study in a single laboratory.

**UK Biobank** — medical, genetic, and cognitive data from 500,000 participants recruited across the United Kingdom since 2006. Longitudinal follow-up means the same individuals return every few years, allowing researchers to detect how brain structure and cognitive function change with age, lifestyle, or disease.

## 4. Why Scale Matters

Small samples constrain what questions scientists can answer. With 20 participants, a study can detect only very large effects — subtle cognitive differences between groups remain invisible. With 10,000 participants, researchers can detect small effects, stratify by age, sex, education, and genetics simultaneously, and identify phenomena that occur in only 1% of the population.

Scale also changes the nature of questions. When data comes from thousands of diverse participants, researchers ask not just "does this effect exist?" but "for whom does it exist, under what conditions, and how large is it?" This shift — from existence questions to characterisation questions — defines modern cognitive data science.

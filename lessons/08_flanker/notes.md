## Expected Observations

The typical student pattern is a noticeable RT difference between congruent and incongruent conditions (20–60 ms range for this age group) combined with nearly perfect accuracy on congruent trials and 2–8% errors on incongruent trials. Students who cannot perceive the RT difference subjectively often find the accuracy difference more intuitive — they "feel" more uncertainty on incongruent trials.

Students comparing Easy (3-second window) and Hard (1-second window) conditions will nearly always report higher error rates under Hard. A productive observation is whether their compatibility effect changes in milliseconds or only in accuracy. Some students will report that under the Hard condition they stopped trying to resolve the conflict and simply responded quickly — this is a genuine strategy shift and connects directly to speed-accuracy trade-off theory.

Some students will spontaneously notice that the compatibility effect seems larger earlier in the session and smaller later. If this comes up, it is an excellent opportunity to introduce practice effects and, if time allows, sequential effects (the Gratton effect).

## Discussion Question Guidance

**Question 1 — The Flanker compatibility effect is much smaller than the Stroop effect (20–80 ms vs. 100–300 ms). What does this tell you about the nature of automatic processing?**
Expected answer: the flanker task relies on spatial priming — a partially automatic process. Arrow directions are processed because they fall within the attentional spotlight, not because they are as thoroughly automatized as reading. The Stroop effect is larger because reading is a fully automatic, over-practised skill that cannot be suppressed. The size difference tells us something fundamental: automaticity is not binary (automatic vs. non-automatic) but exists on a continuum, and tasks can be used to estimate where a given process falls on that continuum.

**Question 2 — After an incongruent trial, the next incongruent trial shows less interference (the Gratton effect). What does this tell us about the nature of attention?**
Expected answer: attention is not a fixed spotlight with a fixed size. The brain continuously adjusts the width of the attentional focus based on recent experience. After experiencing conflict, the system narrows its focus to reduce the influence of irrelevant flankers on the next trial. This is evidence for adaptive, dynamic cognitive control. The Gratton effect is a concrete example of how the brain "learns" within a session — not by forming explicit memories, but by adjusting attentional parameters automatically.

**Question 3 — A child in primary school and an elderly adult both show larger flanker effects than a young adult. But for different reasons — what are those reasons?**
Expected answer: in children, the flanker effect is large because inhibitory control is developmentally immature — the prefrontal control systems that suppress flanker influence are not yet fully developed. In older adults, the flanker effect is large because inhibitory control declines with age — the same prefrontal systems become less efficient. The phenomenological outcome (larger flanker effect) is the same, but the underlying mechanism is different: developmental incompleteness vs. age-related decline.

**Question 4 — How would you design an experiment to test whether meditation reduces the flanker effect?**
Expected answer: a rigorous design would need: (a) random assignment to a meditation vs. active control condition, (b) pre- and post-intervention ANT or flanker measurements, (c) a control group who receives a different intervention matched for time and expectation effects. Existing studies (Tang et al., 2007; Jha et al., 2007) have found meditation-related improvements in executive attention, but causal claims require randomised controlled designs. This question opens discussion of experimental design principles.

## Common Misconceptions

**"A larger flanker effect means worse attention overall."**
The flanker effect measures specifically executive control — the ability to suppress conflicting spatial information. A person with a large flanker effect might have excellent sustained attention (not measured by the flanker task at all) and good alerting and orienting (measured separately in the ANT). The flanker effect is one narrow aspect of attention, not a global attention index. Correct by distinguishing the three attention networks from the ANT framework.

**"The flankers are distracting because they are nearby and hard to ignore perceptually."**
The flanker effect is not simply a perceptual crowding effect. Studies have shown that even when flankers are clearly visible and spatially separated, they produce interference — because they activate a competing response, not because they are perceptually indistinguishable from the target. The interference is post-perceptual: it occurs at the response selection stage, after both the target and flankers have been perceived. Correct by pointing to response-level conflict as distinct from perceptual confusion.

**"Practicing the flanker task will eventually make the effect disappear entirely."**
Extended practice substantially reduces overall RT and also somewhat reduces the flanker compatibility effect. However, the compatibility effect does not reach zero with practice in healthy adults — the spatial priming from flankers persists because it is not as deeply automatised as reading (which, recall, also cannot be fully eliminated by training). A small residual effect is expected even in highly practiced participants.

## Connection to Neighbouring Lessons

- **Lesson 07 (Stroop Challenge):** The Flanker is best understood in comparison with the Stroop. Both measure response conflict; the key differences (size of effect, source of automaticity, clinical sensitivity) are a productive discussion frame. Ask explicitly: are these the same cognitive process or different ones?
- **Lesson 02 (Reaction Time Lab):** Students encounter the concept of RT as a dependent variable early. Flanker extends this by showing that RT differences between conditions, not just absolute RTs, are scientifically meaningful.

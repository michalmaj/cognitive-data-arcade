## Expected Observations

Most students will find 1-Back easy to near-trivial (accuracy often >90%) and 2-Back noticeably harder. Some students will be surprised to find that 2-Back feels qualitatively different rather than just quantitatively harder — this is the signature of the non-linear load increase. Use this to motivate the distinction between near-threshold performance and suprathreshold performance.

In Adaptive mode, students who start with high WM capacity will be pushed to n=3 quickly. Students with lower capacity may stabilise at n=1 or n=2. Neither outcome is cause for concern — the within-student comparison (improvement across sessions) is more meaningful than the absolute level. Emphasise this: stable performance at n=2 with high accuracy is not "worse" than unstable performance at n=3 with 55% accuracy.

Students who play mobile games that require tracking multiple targets simultaneously (tower defence, real-time strategy) often report finding the position component of n-back easier than the letter component. This is an anecdotally useful illustration of individual variation in which WM subsystem is more developed.

## Discussion Question Guidance

**Question 1 — What is working memory, and how is it different from short-term memory?**
Key distinction: STM is passive storage; WM is active maintenance *plus* manipulation. Give the classic example: holding a phone number in mind while walking to another room (STM). Holding the same number while having a conversation and then writing it down when you arrive (WM). The manipulative component — keeping things active while doing something else — is what distinguishes WM.

**Question 2 — Why did the Jaeggi (2008) result fail to replicate?**
Several methodological issues to surface: (1) Active vs. passive control group — participants in the active control group knew they were getting "brain training" and may have been motivated differently; expectation effects alone can produce IQ test gains. (2) The Gf measure (Raven's matrices) shows large practice effects — participants who took the test twice simply improved due to familiarity, not WM training. (3) Small sample sizes in the original study (~35 per group) — insufficient power to detect whether effects generalise. (4) Publication bias: positive results in training studies are published; null results are not. Push students to see this as a lesson in methodology, not just a failed claim.

**Question 3 — If WM training doesn't improve Gf, does that mean WM training is useless?**
Important nuance: near-transfer is real and potentially valuable. A patient with working memory deficits after a stroke who trains on n-back tasks may recover functional WM capacity useful in daily life, even if their Raven's score does not change. The question is whether the training transfers to anything the patient actually cares about. This is a good segue into ecological validity.

**Question 4 — What does the dlPFC do in n-back, and why does damage to it impair the task specifically?**
The dlPFC maintains goal-relevant representations against distraction. In n-back terms, it holds the buffer (the sequence of n recent items) while new items are presented and processed. Damage disrupts the maintenance function — items drop out of the buffer before they are used for comparison. This is *not* a retrieval failure (like in amnesia) but a maintenance failure.

**Question 5 — Why might children with ADHD perform poorly on n-back?**
ADHD involves deficits in the central executive specifically — updating, inhibiting, and shifting. N-back taxes the update function above all others. Children with ADHD also show stronger mind-wandering, which disrupts the continuous maintenance required. This is an opportunity to revisit Lesson 09 (Go/No-Go) and compare: ADHD impairs both n-back (WM capacity) and Go/No-Go (response inhibition) — both functions are central-executive components.

## Common Misconceptions

- **"N-back measures how good your memory is."** More precisely, it measures working memory — active maintenance and updating under load. It does not measure long-term memory, episodic memory, or the kind of memory tested in a trivia quiz.
- **"Higher n always means you're smarter."** N-back performance varies with practice, fatigue, motivation, and strategy, not just capacity. A student who played for 10 sessions will perform better than a naive student at the same underlying WM level.
- **"The Jaeggi paper was fraudulent."** The study was likely conducted honestly — the problem was insufficient power, methodological artifacts, and the extraordinary nature of the claim. This is a lesson about the difference between a well-intentioned study and a replicable finding. Contrast with cases of outright fraud (Diederik Stapel) to distinguish fraud from methodological weakness.
- **"The phonological loop and visuospatial sketchpad are separate brain regions."** They are separate functional systems with distinct neural correlates (left vs. right parietal), but they share the central executive and are integrated at the level of the episodic buffer. The components are not anatomically isolated modules.
- **"WM capacity is fixed from birth."** WM capacity develops through childhood, shows modest training effects, and declines with age. It is also sensitive to sleep deprivation, stress, and acute pharmacological interventions. The capacity of an individual at a given moment is not a fixed trait.

## Connection to Other Lessons

- **Lesson 09 (Go/No-Go):** Both lessons measure executive function. N-back taxes updating; Go/No-Go taxes inhibition. Both involve the central executive. Use this to introduce the unity-and-diversity framework for executive function (Miyake et al., 2000) — three separable but correlated components: updating, inhibiting, shifting.
- **Lesson 02 (RT Lab):** N-back reaction times are longer and more variable than simple RT. The additional processing required for comparison and update adds latency. This is a within-student empirical comparison students can make.

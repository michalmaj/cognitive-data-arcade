## Expected Observations

Most students will find the incongruent condition noticeably harder to perform, even before seeing their RT data. Students who are fast readers often report the strongest subjective experience of interference. This is a good entry point: ask students who felt it most strongly whether they think their RT data will confirm their subjective experience.

Some students will show near-zero or even negative facilitation effects — their neutral and congruent RTs may be close or reversed. Emphasise that this is not a sign of an error: facilitation varies more across individuals than interference does, and a student who is very practiced at the colour-naming task may show minimal congruency benefit.

Students with a second language background may spontaneously mention that the interference felt different or smaller when the colour words were in a language they know less fluently. This is a live demonstration of the bilingual advantage — encourage those students to share the observation.

## Discussion Question Guidance

**Question 1 — Why can you not simply choose to ignore the word?**
Expected answer: the word-reading route is automatic — it operates without intention and cannot be switched off. A common wrong answer is "because the word is more salient." Push back: salience would predict neutral stimuli (XXXXX) to be as fast as words, which is not the case. The asymmetry between conditions requires an account based on differential automaticity of the two processing routes.

**Question 2 — If you practised the task every day for a month, what would happen to your Stroop effect?**
Expected answer: the total Stroop effect would decrease but would not disappear. This leads to a productive discussion: what is being practiced — better inhibition, faster colour naming, or both? Research suggests both contribute, but the automatic reading process does not itself become slower. Ask students what type of training they think would maximally reduce the effect.

**Question 3 — A neuropsychologist uses the Stroop test with a patient who has had a stroke affecting the left frontal lobe. What pattern of results would you predict?**
Expected answer: enlarged interference effect with relatively intact baseline RT. The frontal lobes implement inhibitory control; a lesion reduces the ability to suppress the reading response, amplifying the conflict cost. Some students may predict a global slowdown — this is partially correct (overall RT often increases) but the key clinical signature is the disproportionate enlargement of the interference component.

**Question 4 — The emotional Stroop produces interference without ink-colour conflict. How does this challenge the dual-route model?**
Expected answer: the dual-route model explains interference between two response-generating routes (reading vs. colour naming). The emotional Stroop implies that emotional significance alone can delay colour naming — suggesting that the interference mechanism is broader than just reading automaticity. This is a productive opportunity to discuss whether "automaticity" applies to emotional processing and what that would imply for theories of emotion regulation.

## Common Misconceptions

**"The Stroop effect proves that we are generally bad at multitasking."**
This overgeneralises. The Stroop effect is specifically about the mandatory automaticity of reading — a highly over-practised skill. It does not generalise to all dual-task situations. Many dual-task paradigms show minimal interference when the two tasks use different sensory or motor channels. Correct by anchoring the explanation in the specific overlap between the word-reading and colour-naming response pathways.

**"A larger Stroop effect means a less intelligent person."**
The Stroop effect is not a general intelligence measure. It is sensitive to specific executive function processes, particularly response inhibition. High-IQ individuals do not systematically show smaller Stroop effects than average-IQ individuals. Individual differences in the Stroop effect relate most strongly to working memory capacity (Engle et al., 1995) and, in clinical populations, to frontal lobe integrity.

**"The neutral condition (XXXXX) is meaningless — it is just a baseline."**
The neutral condition allows interference and facilitation to be separated. Without it, the total Stroop effect confounds two distinct processes: the cost of suppressing an incongruent response and the benefit of a congruent response. Many studies omit the neutral condition and report only the total effect — this is an important methodological limitation worth highlighting.

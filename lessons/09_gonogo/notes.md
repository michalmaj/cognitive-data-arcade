## Expected Observations

Most students will have commission error rates between 8–20% at Medium difficulty. Students who play video games regularly often report lower commission error rates — this is an interesting discussion point about whether gaming trains response inhibition or simply reflects pre-existing executive function differences.

At Hard difficulty, some students see lower commission error rates despite a harder overall impression. The reason: 60% Go means there are proportionally more No-Go trials, which breaks the prepotency. The shorter response window (0.7 s) adds pressure but in a different direction. This can confuse students — use it to distinguish Go ratio effects from time pressure effects.

Commission error rate tends to rise in later blocks within a session. This fatigue/depletion effect is not always visible in a single 80-trial session, but students who look carefully at their trial-by-trial data may notice it.

## Discussion Question Guidance

**Question 1 — What is the difference between a commission error and an omission error?**
Students often say a commission error is "pressing when you shouldn't" and an omission error is "not pressing when you should." Push further: why is the commission error specifically a measure of *inhibitory control* rather than attention? The key insight is that a commission error reflects the *failure to suppress* an already-prepared response — the motor program started. An omission error reflects a failure of alertness or detection, not a failure of stopping.

**Question 2 — Two students both have 85% accuracy. Can one be impulsive and the other inattentive?**
This is the core SDT question. Student A: 95% hit rate, 15% FA rate — impulsive. Student B: 75% hit rate, 5% FA rate — cautious or inattentive. Both could be at 85% overall accuracy if the Go:No-Go ratio is approximately 3:1. Calculate their d' values with the class: d'_A ≈ 2.33 − (−1.04) = 1.29... wait — use Z(0.95) = 1.64, Z(0.15) = −1.04: d'_A = 1.64 − (−1.04) = 2.68. For B: Z(0.75) = 0.67, Z(0.05) = −1.64: d'_B = 0.67 − (−1.64) = 2.31. Both show good discrimination despite the accuracy difference — the comparison shows how overall accuracy can be misleading.

**Question 3 — Why does inhibitory control develop slowly?**
Expected answers: prefrontal cortex maturation. Push students to think about *why* this is adaptive — having an impulsive, fast-responding child may have evolutionary advantages (react quickly to threats). The slow development of deliberate inhibition may be the more recent evolutionary feature.

**Question 4 — How does the Stop-Signal Task differ from Go/No-Go?**
Key distinction: in SST, the Go response has *already started* when the stop signal arrives. The participant is cancelling an in-flight motor program, not preventing one from starting. Use the analogy of a car: Go/No-Go is deciding not to start the engine vs. SST is braking after you've already begun to drive.

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Fewer commission errors always means better performance."** A participant who responds on only 50% of Go trials will have very few commission errors but also terrible hit rate and low d'. Performance must be evaluated jointly — commission errors and hit rate together, ideally as d'.
- **"No-Go trials are easier because you don't have to do anything."** Correct rejections require active inhibition — they are cognitively demanding. ERP studies show larger N2 components on No-Go than Go trials, reflecting the additional neural work.
- **"ADHD means you're always impulsive."** ADHD is heterogeneous. Some presentations are predominantly inattentive (high omission errors, slow RT) rather than hyperactive-impulsive (high commission errors). Go/No-Go helps distinguish these profiles.
- **"d' is just another accuracy measure."** d' is bias-free; accuracy is not. Two participants with the same d' can have very different accuracy if one responds much more liberally than the other.

## Connection to Other Lessons

- **Lesson 02 (RT Lab):** Students can compare their Go trial reaction times with their simple RT from Lesson 02. The Go/No-Go typically produces slower RTs on Go trials — adding the inhibitory demand increases response caution (a slowing effect on Go RT is a well-known consequence of increased inhibitory context).
- **Lesson 08 (Flanker):** Both Flanker and Go/No-Go measure inhibitory control but at different stages. Flanker measures the ability to ignore competing response-relevant information (conflict monitoring); Go/No-Go measures the ability to suppress a prepared response entirely. Introduce the distinction between *conflict monitoring* and *response inhibition* as different executive function components.
